\begin{abstract}
Black-box testing is a critical quality assurance practice, yet it remains labor-intensive, error-prone, and heavily dependent on tester expertise. This study conducts a rigorous empirical comparison of test suites generated by state-of-the-art Large Language Models (LLMs)—specifically GPT-5.5, Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Sonnet 4.6), Gemini 3.1, and ChatGPT-4—against human-authored suites from two distinct cohorts: professional developers and third-year software engineering students. We replicated the baseline methodology of Kirinuki and Tanno across three applications (Password Strength Checker, Unit Converter, Budget Planner) and extended it to two new CLI applications (Library Fine Calculator, Task Manager) with student testers. Test viewpoints were extracted and mapped against a unified matrix, and coverage was measured using five quantitative metrics. Statistical significance was validated using an exact two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test computed via dynamic programming. Results show that modern LLMs significantly outperform human testers in viewpoint coverage (exact $p = 0.00002$ across all categories), yet human testers contribute unique boundary viewpoints not found by any AI. The Human+AI combined union achieves 98.1\%–100\% coverage, confirming strong synergistic potential for collaborative QA workflows.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background}
Black-box testing (specification-based testing) is a foundational software quality assurance (QA) practice in which test cases are designed purely from behavioral specifications, without knowledge of the internal implementation. This approach ensures that all externally observable behaviors—including boundary values, invalid inputs, error handling, and state transitions—are exercised by the test suite.

Traditionally, black-box test design has been entirely manual. Experienced testers analyze requirements documents, identify equivalence classes, boundary conditions, and error scenarios, then craft test cases accordingly. This process is extremely time-consuming: in our study, professional developers spent an average of 197.75 minutes across three applications (averaging 60.25 minutes for Password Checker, 63.25 minutes for Unit Converter, and 74.25 minutes for Budget Planner), yet still missed a significant fraction of viewpoints.

The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on vast code repositories and natural language corpora has opened an unprecedented opportunity. These models can read a requirements specification and instantaneously generate comprehensive, structured test cases in natural language or code. 

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Three core problems motivate this research:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Stale Benchmarks on Older Models:} The baseline study benchmarked ChatGPT (GPT-4). Newer-generation models, such as GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, have not been rigorously benchmarked against identical human baselines under the same specifications and viewpoint extraction methodology.
    \item \textit{Undifferentiated Human Baselines:} Most comparative studies treat all human testers as a single baseline. Conflating a professional developer with years of QA experience and a third-year student taking their first software testing course obscures important insights regarding different cognitive testing strategies.
    \item \textit{Unmeasured Synergy:} Even when studies show that LLMs achieve higher individual coverage, they rarely quantify whether the two sources complement each other. Quantifying the union (Human+AI combined) coverage is critical to determining how much the combined suite closes the gap toward 100\% coverage.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}
Our systematic literature review followed the PRISMA framework, searching arXiv, IEEE Xplore, and ACM Digital Library for terms related to LLM test generation and specification-based testing. 

Kirinuki and Tanno (2024) benchmarked GPT-4 test generation against four human professionals using viewpoint coverage, reporting that ChatGPT matches or slightly surpasses non-expert humans, while professionals catch unique boundary cases. Arora et al. (2024) evaluated retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) for natural language test-scenario generation in an industrial setting, noting that RAG improves domain accuracy. Sami et al. (2024) constructed a React, Flask, and OpenAI-based web tool to generate Gherkin scenarios, though they found the models struggled with complex state logic. 

Furthermore, the TestGenEval benchmark (2024) evaluated LLM unit-test authoring on real repositories, demonstrating that the best model (GPT-4o) averaged only 35.2\% statement coverage. Ferreira et al. (2025) explored Cypress acceptance test generation, finding that while the AI tool was helpful 95\% of the time, it frequently missed edge cases. 

The primary research gap remains that existing work evaluates older models, mixes professional and student baselines indiscriminately, and fails to rigorously compute the mathematical union of AI and human coverage.

\section{Research Scope and Methodology}

\subsection{Data Preparation and Research Scope}
This study evaluates five applications organized into two cohorts that are analyzed and reported \emph{separately} throughout, since they differ both in data provenance and in human-tester profile. The \textbf{replicated cohort} consists of the Password Strength Checker, Unit Converter, and Budget Planner applications, using the original specifications, the original four professional developers' test suites (Human A--D), and the original ChatGPT (GPT-4) test suite from Kirinuki and Tanno --- all reused unchanged as the human/GPT-4 baseline. This study's own contribution to that cohort is generating three \emph{additional} LLM test suites (GPT-5.5, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 3.1) and scoring them against the same, untouched baseline; no new human data was collected for these three applications.

The \textbf{extended cohort} includes two new CLI applications designed for this study: the Library Fine Calculator and Task Manager. Here, both the AI suites and the human suites are entirely original: three third-year software engineering students (Student A--C) tested independently without AI assistance, against three modern LLMs (Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1, ChatGPT-5.5) run fresh for this study. A consistent zero-shot prompt template was used for all newly-generated models, explicitly instructing them to act as expert QA engineers and focus on boundary values, equivalence classes, error handling, and state transitions without writing implementation code. Because of this provenance difference, the replicated and extended cohorts are never pooled into a single AI-vs-human statistic; every table in Section~IV reports them separately.

\subsection{Evaluation Pipeline and Metrics}
Test viewpoints were manually extracted and mapped into a single unified matrix. We define the following coverage measurement formulas:
\begin{equation}
    Cov = \frac{N_{covered}}{N_{total}} \times 100\%
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    Miss = \frac{N_{total} - N_{covered}}{N_{total}} \times 100\%
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    Union = \frac{\left| \{\, v \mid \exists s \in (Human \cup AI),\, covered(v,s) \,\} \right|}{N_{total}} \times 100\%
\end{equation}

\subsection{Statistical Hypothesis Testing}
To validate the statistical significance of AI versus human coverage differences, we utilized the Wilcoxon signed-rank test with exact $p$-values computed via Dynamic Programming. Let $d_i = Cov_{AI,i} - Cov_{Human,i}$ for each category $i$. After filtering ties, we rank $|d_i|$ and compute:
\begin{equation}
    W^+ = \sum_{d_i > 0} \text{rank}(|d_i|), \quad W^- = \sum_{d_i < 0} \text{rank}(|d_i|)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    W = \min(W^+, W^-)
\end{equation}
The exact $p$-value is computed using a 0/1-knapsack DP over all $2^{N_{usable}}$ sign combinations, where ranks are doubled to handle half-ranks, and the DP transition is $dp[s] \mathrel{+}= dp[s - r_j]$.

\section{Threats to Validity and Limitations}
Several limitations impact this study:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Subjectivity in Viewpoint Extraction:} The mapping of raw test case text to discrete viewpoints was manual.
    \item \textit{Scale of Applications:} All five applications are small-scale CLI tools. 
    \item \textit{Viewpoint Coverage vs. Fault Detection:} This study measures whether a requirement aspect was addressed, not whether the test would catch a real bug.
    \item \textit{Student Cohort Size:} With only three students, the extreme variance observed may not generalize.
    \item \textit{LLM Non-Determinism:} LLM outputs are non-deterministic, and our data relies on a single sampling per model.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
Modern models significantly outperform human testers in viewpoint coverage (exact $p = 0.00002$). However, the extreme variance in the student cohort and the persistent ability of experienced humans to catch unique boundary cases underscore the need for collaboration. Because the Human+AI combined union reaches 98.1\%--100\% coverage, we propose a two-phase QA workflow: leveraging LLMs to generate a comprehensive baseline, followed by targeted human review.
